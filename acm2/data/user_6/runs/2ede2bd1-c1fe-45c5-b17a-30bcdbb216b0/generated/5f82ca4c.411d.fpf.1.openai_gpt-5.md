Executive summary
- What it is: Executive Order 14201—Keeping Men Out of Women’s Sports—was signed on February 5, 2025 and published in the Federal Register on February 11, 2025. It directs federal agencies to ensure that women’s sports are reserved for females, to review and, where appropriate, rescind federal funding to non‑compliant education programs, and to press domestic and international sports bodies to adopt sex‑based eligibility rules. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Why it matters: The order anchors federal policy to a biological‑sex reading of Title IX, cites federal court decisions vacating the 2024 Title IX rule, and has already prompted enforcement actions by federal agencies and rapid policy shifts by major athletic organizations. [1][3][4][5] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))

Document provenance and text
- Official publication: Federal Register, FR Doc. 2025‑02513, “Keeping Men Out of Women’s Sports,” 90 Fed. Reg. 9279 (Feb. 11, 2025). [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Archival copy: The American Presidency Project reproduces the text and notes the Feb. 11 Federal Register publication. [2] ([presidency.ucsb.edu](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/executive-order-14201-keeping-men-out-womens-sports))

Key legal context referenced by the order
- Title IX rule vacatur: The order points to litigation against the Department of Education’s April 29, 2024 Title IX rule (89 FR 33474) and relies on federal court decisions vacating or enjoining that rule, including Tennessee v. Cardona (E.D. Ky. Jan. 9, 2025) and Kansas v. U.S. Department of Education (D. Kan. July 2, 2024). [1][3][4][5] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Department of Education stance: Following the vacatur, ED confirmed a return to enforcing the 2020 Title IX rule. [3][5] ([mcguirewoods.com](https://www.mcguirewoods.com/client-resources/alerts/2025/1/title-ix-final-rule-vacated-nationwide/?utm_source=openai))

Core provisions of Executive Order 14201
- Section 1 (Policy): Establishes U.S. policy to rescind federal funds from educational programs that deprive women and girls of fair athletic opportunities and to oppose male participation in women’s sports. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Section 2 (Definitions): Incorporates definitions from EO 14168 (Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism and Restoring Biological Truth to the Federal Government). [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Section 3 (Education/Title IX):
  - Directs the Secretary of Education, with DOJ, to comply with the vacatur of the 2024 Title IX rule and ensure it has no effect. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Orders prioritization of Title IX enforcement against institutions or athletic associations that, in women’s categories, require females to compete with or against males or to appear unclothed before males. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Requires agencies to review grants and, where appropriate, rescind funding to programs that fail to comply. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- Section 4 (Sports governance, international, immigration):
  - Requires the White House Domestic Policy staff to convene major athletic organizations and state attorneys general to promote policies consistent with Title IX. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Directs the Secretary of State to rescind support for programs where women’s categories are based on identity rather than sex, and to promote sex‑based rules internationally (including at the United Nations). [1][6] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Directs State and DHS to review admission policies and issue guidance to prevent entry of males seeking to compete in women’s sports, referencing INA §212(a)(6)(C)(i) (fraud/misrepresentation). [1][6] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Directs State to use available measures to seek IOC standards that determine eligibility for women’s events by sex, not gender identity or testosterone levels. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))

Federal implementation to date (selected)
- HHS Office for Civil Rights opened a Title IX compliance review of Maine on Feb. 21, 2025, citing EO 14201. [7] ([hhs.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/press-room/keeping-men-out-womens-sports.html))
- HHS OCR issued a determination on Mar. 17, 2025 finding Maine entities in violation of Title IX for allowing males in women’s sports and set a 10‑day window to resolve or face DOJ referral. [8] ([hhs.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/press-room/hhs-civil-rights-office-determines-maine-violates-title-ix-allowing-males-womens-sports.html?utm_source=openai))
- Department of Education announced investigations on Feb. 6, 2025 into San José State University, the University of Pennsylvania, and the Massachusetts Interscholastic Athletic Association, pursuant to the order. [9] ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/us-department-of-education-investigate-title-ix-violations-athletics?utm_source=openai))
- USCIS issued guidance on Aug. 4, 2025 aligning immigration adjudications with EO 14201 to prevent male athletes from using U.S. immigration benefits to participate in women’s sports, clarifying several athlete‑related visa categories. [10] ([uscis.gov](https://www.uscis.gov/newsroom/news-releases/uscis-updating-policy-to-protect-womens-sports?utm_source=openai))

Downstream impacts in sports governance
- NCAA policy change: On Feb. 6, 2025 the NCAA Board of Governors changed its transgender participation policy, limiting competition in women’s sports to athletes assigned female at birth; this was announced one day after EO 14201. [11][12] ([ncaa.org](https://www.ncaa.org/news/2025/2/6/media-center-ncaa-announces-transgender-student-athlete-participation-policy-change.aspx?utm_source=openai))
- Media and industry coverage confirm the NCAA shift and its immediate effect across all member schools, replacing the prior sport‑by‑sport approach. [12][16] ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/0850b452de89674aeac7c6b1d8bce6d5?utm_source=openai))

Interaction with ongoing litigation and policy environment
- The Supreme Court heard arguments on Jan. 13, 2026 in consolidated cases from Idaho and West Virginia concerning state bans on transgender athletes; initial reporting suggested the Court was likely to uphold such bans, indicating a permissive environment for sex‑based eligibility rules. A decision is expected by early summer 2026. [16] ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a0e50014fbf7f3ef5b1d1e9b5e8b662d?utm_source=openai))
- Broader legal challenges to related administration policies (including EO 14168, which supplies definitions used by EO 14201) have been filed by civil‑rights organizations alleging constitutional violations; these suits challenge the policy framework into which EO 14201 explicitly plugs. [13][14][15] ([naacpldf.org](https://www.naacpldf.org/press-release/civil-and-human-rights-organizations-sue-trump-administration-over-executive-orders-banning-diversity-equity-inclusion-accessibility-and-erasing-transgender-people/?utm_source=openai))

Operational implications and compliance takeaways
- For federally funded education institutions:
  - Review and, if necessary, revise athletic eligibility and facilities policies to ensure women’s teams, competitions, and sex‑segregated spaces are reserved for females, consistent with EO 14201 and current ED enforcement posture, or face potential grant rescission and enforcement actions. [1][3][5][9] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
  - Monitor agency investigations and OCR determinations (e.g., Maine) as indicators of enforcement priorities and remedial expectations. [7][8] ([hhs.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/press-room/keeping-men-out-womens-sports.html))
- For national and international sports bodies:
  - Expect White House and State Department engagement pressing for sex‑based eligibility standards in women’s categories; organizations with U.S. governmental relationships or funding ties are likely focal points. [1][6] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
- For immigration stakeholders (athletes, sponsors, universities, teams):
  - USCIS guidance indicates closer scrutiny of athlete‑related petitions and a stated goal to prevent male athletes from using immigration benefits to compete in women’s sports, with potential fraud/misrepresentation implications under INA §212(a)(6)(C)(i). [1][10] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html))
