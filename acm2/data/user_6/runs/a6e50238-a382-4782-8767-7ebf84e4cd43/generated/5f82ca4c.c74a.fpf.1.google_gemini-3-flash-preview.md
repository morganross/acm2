Executive Order 14201, titled "**Keeping Men Out of Women's Sports**," was signed by President Donald J. Trump on **February 5, 2025** [1][3]. The order establishes a federal policy that defines "sex" in the context of Title IX and collegiate athletics based on biological sex assigned at birth, effectively prohibiting transgender women and girls from participating in female sports at all educational levels [4][9].

### **Core Objectives and Policy**
The stated purpose of the order is to protect athletic opportunities for women and girls by ensuring they do not have to compete against or share private spaces with "biological males" [3][13]. The order asserts that allowing transgender women to participate in women's sports is "demeaning, unfair, and dangerous" and deprives biological women of "safety, fairness, dignity, and truth" [3][9].

Key policy declarations include:
*   **Funding Rescission:** The United States will rescind federal funds from any educational program that allows transgender women or girls to compete on female sports teams [2][9].
*   **Title IX Reinterpretation:** The order directs the Department of Education to interpret Title IX of the Education Amendments Act of 1972 as protecting only biological females, reversing Biden-era interpretations that included gender identity [4][12].
*   **Privacy Protections:** It mandates the protection of "all-female locker rooms" and directs agencies to prioritize enforcement against schools that require female students to "appear unclothed before males" [2][6].

### **Key Provisions and Definitions**
*   **Definitions via EO 14168:** The order adopts the definitions of "male" and "female" established in **Executive Order 14168**, "Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism and Restoring Biological Truth to the Federal Government" [3][5]. These definitions rely on "immutable biological classification" at the time of birth [5].
*   **International Travel Restrictions:** Section 4(c) instructs the Secretaries of State and Homeland Security to adjust policies to prevent the entry of transgender women (referred to as "males") into the U.S. if they are seeking to participate in women's sports competitions [7].
*   **Coordination with State Officials:** The order directs the Attorney General to convene State Attorneys General to identify "best practices" for enforcing these standards and to document "stories of women and girls who have been harmed" by transgender participation [3][13].

### **Immediate Impact and Implementation**
The issuance of EO 14201 triggered immediate changes across national and international sports organizations:
*   **NCAA Policy Shift:** On February 6, 2025—the day after the order was signed—the **NCAA** revised its participation policy to limit women's sports competitions exclusively to athletes assigned female at birth [1][9].
*   **Visa Marking ('SWS25'):** The State Department announced a tracking system for visa applicants suspected of seeking entry for women's sports competitions, reportedly marking files with the letters "**SWS25**" [1][7].
*   **Department of Education Probes:** The administration immediately opened investigations into various school districts and colleges, including New York City’s school system and San Jose State University, for potential Title IX violations related to transgender inclusion [10][11].

### **Legal Challenges and Reception**
The order has faced immediate legal scrutiny and a mixed public reception:
*   **Litigation:** On February 12, 2025, two transgender high school students in New Hampshire filed a lawsuit against the administration, alleging that EO 14201 violates the U.S. Constitution and Title IX [4].
*   **Supreme Court Context:** The order coincides with Supreme Court deliberations on state-level bans (e.g., Idaho's *Fairness in Women's Sports Act* and West Virginia's *Save Women's Sports Act*), with the administration filing "friend of the court" briefs supporting such bans [16][17].
*   **Support and Criticism:** Proponents, including various state officials and athletic advocacy groups, praise the order for restoring "biological truth" to sports [3][13]. Critics argue it constitutes a "broader targeting of transgender people" and violates the precedent set in *Bostock v. Clayton County*, which recognized gender identity as protected under sex-based discrimination laws [1][4].
