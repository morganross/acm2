Executive summary
- Executive Order 14201—Keeping Men Out of Women’s Sports—was signed on February 5, 2025, and published in the Federal Register on February 11, 2025 (90 FR 9279). The order states that, to protect “safe and fair” competition for women and girls and to enforce Title IX, federal agencies must withhold or rescind funding from educational programs that allow “men” to compete in women’s sports. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- EO 14201 builds on—and incorporates the definitions from—Executive Order 14168 (January 20, 2025), which directs the executive branch to recognize only two immutable sexes (male and female) and to apply sex-based terms accordingly in federal policy and enforcement. [3][2] ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/defending-women-from-gender-ideology-extremism-and-restoring-biological-truth-to-the-federal-government/?utm_source=openai))
- The order expressly references the January 9, 2025 nationwide vacatur of the Department of Education’s 2024 Title IX rule in State of Tennessee v. Cardona and aligns federal Title IX enforcement with that decision. [1][2][12][13][14] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))

What the order does (structure and key directives)
- Section 1 (Policy and Purpose): Declares it U.S. policy to rescind federal funds from educational programs that “deprive women and girls of fair athletic opportunities” by allowing males in women’s categories; frames this as required by Title IX. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- Section 2 (Definitions): Incorporates the definitions in EO 14168 (e.g., “sex” means biological male or female; not “gender identity”). [1][3] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- Section 3 (Education/Title IX enforcement):
  - Directs the Secretary of Education, in coordination with the Attorney General, to “comply with the vacatur” of the April 29, 2024 Title IX rule and to take actions to “affirmatively protect” all‑female sports and locker rooms. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
  - Directs all agencies to review grants to educational programs and, “where appropriate,” rescind funding for noncompliance. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
  - Directs DOJ to provide resources to ensure enforcement. [1] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- Section 4 (Beyond education):
  - Directs the White House Domestic Policy staff to convene major athletic bodies and affected female athletes within 60 days to promote policies “fair and safe” for female athletes. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
  - Directs the Department of State and DHS to review admission/visa policies to “prevent the entry” of males seeking to compete in women’s sports where permitted by law; State is also directed to use available measures to push the International Olympic Committee (IOC) to ensure women’s categories are based on sex, not gender identity or testosterone thresholds. [1][4] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- Section 5 (General Provisions): Standard clauses on lawful implementation, appropriations, severability, and no private right of action. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))

Relationship to Title IX and the 2024 Title IX rule
- EO 14201 relies on the nationwide vacatur of the 2024 Title IX regulation by the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Kentucky in State of Tennessee v. Cardona (Jan. 9, 2025), which set aside the rule as contrary to law, unconstitutional, and arbitrary and capricious; multiple legal advisories and news reports confirm the vacatur’s nationwide effect. [12][13][14] ([politico.com](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/01/09/federal-judge-vacates-bidens-title-ix-rule-00197353?utm_source=openai))
- The order repeatedly cites that vacatur to instruct Education and DOJ on enforcement posture; it also cites a Kansas case challenging the 2024 rule (Kansas v. U.S. Dept. of Education). [1][2][15] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- EO 14168 (incorporated by EO 14201) directs the executive branch to interpret sex as biological sex in all federal operations, which frames how agencies apply Title IX following Cardona. [3] ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/defending-women-from-gender-ideology-extremism-and-restoring-biological-truth-to-the-federal-government/?utm_source=openai))

Federal implementation and early agency actions
- Department of Education: On February 5, 2025, the Department issued a press statement supporting EO 14201 and prioritizing Title IX enforcement against institutions that do not protect women’s single‑sex sports and locker rooms. [7] ([ed.gov](https://www.ed.gov/about/news/press-release/statement-president-trumps-executive-order-keeping-men-out-of-womens-sports?utm_source=openai))
- Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Rights (OCR): Announced a compliance review of Maine’s education authorities for potential noncompliance and explicitly tied its actions to EO 14201’s funding‑rescission policy. [5] ([hhs.gov](https://www.hhs.gov/press-room/keeping-men-out-womens-sports.html?utm_source=openai))
- Department of Homeland Security/USCIS: Issued guidance “in alignment with Executive Order 14201,” clarifying that certain athlete‑related immigration benefits will be granted “only to women” and that DHS will act to prevent “male aliens” from being authorized to participate in women’s sports. [6] ([uscis.gov](https://www.uscis.gov/newsroom/news-releases/uscis-updating-policy-to-protect-womens-sports?utm_source=openai))
- White House fact sheet (Feb. 5, 2025) summarized the order’s directives, including DOJ compliance with the Cardona vacatur, immediate enforcement against non‑compliant schools/associations, State‑led IOC engagement, and visa policy review. [4] ([whitehouse.gov](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/02/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-withdraws-from-anti-american-un-organizations/))
- Practitioner resources (e.g., NAFSA) catalogued EO 14201 with its Federal Register citation (90 FR 9279) and summarized its enforcement framework. [8] ([nafsa.org](https://www.nafsa.org/executive-and-regulatory-actions-trump2admin?utm_source=openai))

Observed ripple effects in sports governance (selected examples)
- NCAA: On February 6, 2025, the NCAA adopted a national policy limiting competition in women’s sports to athletes assigned female at birth, replacing its prior sport‑by‑sport framework. Reporting attributes the shift to alignment with EO 14201 and the federal enforcement posture under Title IX. [8][9][10] ([nafsa.org](https://www.nafsa.org/executive-and-regulatory-actions-trump2admin?utm_source=openai))
- U.S. Olympic & Paralympic Committee (USOPC) and NGBs: Subsequent reporting describes moves by USOPC and national governing bodies (e.g., USA Cycling) to restrict participation in women’s categories to those assigned female at birth, with coverage noting alignment with EO 14201 and federal expectations. [11][8] ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/338c43225fdfad936d4b85c1a67ced36?utm_source=openai))

Legal landscape and litigation context as of January 2026
- Supreme Court term: In January 2026, the Supreme Court heard arguments in cases from Idaho and West Virginia involving state bans on transgender girls/women competing in girls’/women’s sports; reporting indicates the Court appeared skeptical of challenges to those bans. Any decision could affect how EO 14201 interacts with state laws and Title IX enforcement. [11] ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a0e50014fbf7f3ef5b1d1e9b5e8b662d?utm_source=openai))
- Ongoing Title IX suits: The Kansas litigation (Kansas v. U.S. Dept. of Education) challenging the 2024 rule continues to appear in case trackers; post‑Cardona developments include narrowed or withdrawn appeals as the Administration reoriented Title IX enforcement in line with EO 14168 and EO 14201. [15][8] ([clearinghouse.net](https://clearinghouse.net/case/45705/?utm_source=openai))

Practical implications for institutions and associations that receive federal funds
- Policy alignment: Schools, districts, colleges, athletic associations composed of or governed by schools, and related entities receiving federal funds face a funding‑contingent obligation under EO 14201’s policy to reserve women’s sports and related facilities to female students as defined by EO 14168. [1][3] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- Enforcement posture: DOJ and Education are directed to prioritize Title IX actions against institutions that require females to compete with or against males in women’s categories or to “appear unclothed before males,” and agencies are directed to review and, where appropriate, rescind grants for noncompliance. [1][2] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
- International/immigration dimensions: State and DHS are to review admission and visa policies to limit entry of male athletes seeking to compete in women’s sports and to press the IOC for rules based on sex rather than gender identity or testosterone levels. [1][4][6] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))

Open questions to monitor
- Supreme Court rulings in the 2025–26 term on state transgender‑athlete bans could recalibrate how courts view Title IX, equal protection, and athletic eligibility policies nationally, influencing EO 14201 enforcement and related agency rules. [11] ([apnews.com](https://apnews.com/article/a0e50014fbf7f3ef5b1d1e9b5e8b662d?utm_source=openai))
- Further agency rulemaking or guidance (Education, HHS, DHS/USCIS, State) implementing EO 14201 may refine compliance details, particularly funding conditions, investigative criteria, and visa adjudications tied to athlete eligibility. [1][4][5][6][7] ([federalregister.gov](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/full_text/html/2025/02/11/2025-02513.html?utm_source=openai))
